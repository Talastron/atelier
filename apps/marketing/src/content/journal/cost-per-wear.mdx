There is a calculation, popular among the thrift-conscious, called cost-per-wear. You take what
a garment cost and divide it by the number of times you have worn it. A coat at £300 worn two
hundred times has cost you £1.50 a wearing; a dress at £30 worn twice has cost you £15. The
arithmetic is meant to console.

But the calculation, examined closely, is not really about money. It is about attention. To know
the cost-per-wear of a garment is to know two things at once: what you paid, and what you have
actually done with it. The first is a figure you knew on the day. The second accumulates quietly,
against your own forgetfulness, until eventually it tells the truth.

It is, in this sense, a moral instrument. The expensive thing you wear constantly grows cheaper
every Tuesday. The cheap thing you wear once grows more expensive every season it hangs unworn.
The number is a small, honest reckoning of whether you chose well.
