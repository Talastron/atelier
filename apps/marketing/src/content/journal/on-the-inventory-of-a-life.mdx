title: 'On the inventory of a life'

description: "Why most of us couldn't name what we own, and why that's the first problem of a wardrobe."

Ask most people to list the clothes they own and they will name fifteen items, perhaps twenty,
before trailing off. The rest of the wardrobe exists as a kind of ambient cloud: present in the
drawer, absent from the mind. We acquire faster than we attend, and the brain, sensibly, declines
to keep score.

This is not so much a failure of memory as a failure of relationship. The unnamed garment is not
quite owned. It is stored, which is a different verb. A wardrobe is meant to be a small library,
walked past each morning with some idea of what sits on its shelves. Most wardrobes are instead a
small warehouse. The distinction matters, because it is the difference between getting dressed
with intention and getting dressed from whichever sleeve presents itself first.

The first work of stewardship is inventory. Not an audit in the accountant's sense, but the slow
act of looking at each thing and saying its name. After that, the larger questions become
possible: what to wear, what to keep, what to let go of. Before that, none of them do.
