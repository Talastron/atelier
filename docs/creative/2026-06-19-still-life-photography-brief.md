# Atelier — Still-Life Photography Commission

**A brief for a single-day still-life shoot. Eight plates. Cream, brass, linen, and quiet daylight.**

| | |
|---|---|
| **Brand** | Atelier — *myatelier.style* |
| **Project** | Visual establishment of the marketing site (homepage, About, Journal) |
| **Issued** | 19 June 2026 |
| **Deliverable** | 8 final retouched plates + selects |
| **Timeline** | Six weeks from brief to delivery |
| **Budget guidance** | £2,000–4,500 inclusive of styling, prop hire, post |

---

## 1. The brand in a sentence

Atelier is a digital studio for the considered wardrobe. We help people inhabit, rather than acquire, their clothes. The brand sits in the editorial tradition of *The Gentlewoman*, *Cereal*, Aesop, Toast, and The Modern House: quiet, restrained, slightly British, materially literate. Nothing about us shouts. Everything about us has been chosen.

## 2. What we are commissioning

A suite of **eight still-life photographs** that will establish the visual world of the brand across the marketing site and the editorial Journal. We are not photographing clothes on people. We are photographing the *materials and tools* of a wardrobe practice — folded knitwear, leather, linen, paper, brass — as if they were the subjects of a quiet interior painting.

The shoot is one day, on a single location (a north-facing room, or a studio set dressed to feel like one). The plates will be used as homepage hero, About-page imagery, Journal masthead plate, and across the site as visual punctuation. They will live alongside the brand's typography (Playfair Display + Jost) and its brass-on-cream palette.

This is not a product shoot. There is nothing to sell in any frame. The photographs should feel like illustrations from a book about how to live with what you own.

## 3. The visual world

Four hallmarks define every frame:

1. **Still life, not lifestyle.** No models. No faces. No hands except in one specifically-noted plate. The relationship being photographed is the relationship between objects and the surfaces they rest on.
2. **North-facing winter daylight.** No strobe. No fill. If anything, the light should be slightly underexposed — the mood of a late-afternoon study, not a sunlit morning. Shadows are soft and long.
3. **Materially literate.** Linen, raw wool, undyed cotton, unfinished oak, honed limestone or pale marble, hand-bound paper, brass (the warm, slightly tarnished kind, not bright gold). These are the materials of every frame, even when they are not the subject.
4. **Generous negative space, magazine crop.** Subjects occupy the lower two-thirds. The top of every frame is breathing room. Compositions are off-centre and quiet; never centred and never busy.

### Tonal palette

The frames sit inside the brand's existing palette:

- Background tones: **cream** (`#F7F5F2`), **stone** (warm grey), the occasional **ink** (off-black) for contrast.
- Accent: **brass** (`#A8884C` to `#D4B378`), used sparingly — a brass paperclip, a thimble, the worn corner of a leather buckle.
- Avoid: any bright colour, any pure white, any cool grey, any plastic, any glass with reflections, any branding visible in frame.

### References (publication register, not exact compositions)

- The opening editorial spreads in *The Gentlewoman* (text-heavy, image-quiet)
- *Cereal Magazine*, particularly the photographic essays on craft and material
- Aesop's product photography (Romain Laprade's register)
- Toast's seasonal catalogues (object-on-linen, soft daylight)
- &Daughter knitwear photography (the close studies of stitches and fibre)
- The Modern House property photography (interior light, restraint)
- Le Labo's editorial work (apothecary-still-life mood)

If a single image had to set the register, it would be a Toast catalogue plate of a folded jumper on a linen tablecloth in a north-light window: utterly composed, technically simple, emotionally exact.

## 4. The shot list

Eight plates. Listed in order of priority — if the day runs short, the first three must be delivered.

### Plate 1 — Hero (homepage replacement)

**Subject:** A stack of three folded knitwear pieces — one cream, one stone, one navy — on a washed linen surface.
**Composition:** ¾ overhead, slightly off-axis. The stack sits in the lower-right third; the upper-left is empty linen with soft shadow.
**Format:** Square (1:1), suitable for cropping to 21:9 if needed.
**Why:** Replaces the current homepage placeholder. Establishes "this is a brand about clothes you keep."

### Plate 2 — Journal cover plate

**Subject:** A wax-seal monogram pressed into a folded sheet of hand-bound paper, a fountain pen lying across, a small linen-bound ledger half-visible.
**Composition:** Tight overhead. Brass wax. Cream paper. The pen runs at a diagonal.
**Format:** 4:5 vertical.
**Why:** Establishes the publication as a serious editorial object. Will sit above or beside the *Atelier Quarterly* masthead.

### Plate 3 — Material study

**Subject:** Three fabric swatches arranged tonally: a heathered grey wool, a vegetable-tanned tan leather, an oat-coloured linen. On honed pale marble or limestone.
**Composition:** Overhead, swatches running diagonal corner to corner. Each material occupies roughly equal frame. Close crop — the edges of the swatches are not all visible.
**Format:** Square.
**Why:** About page header. Establishes the brand's material vocabulary in one frame.

### Plate 4 — The tailor's hand

**Subject:** A pair of hands (any age, any gender — we want a quietly skilled pair, not a glamorous one) drawing a tailor's chalk line on a piece of dark wool. A measuring tape coiled beside, a brass thimble in soft focus on the edge.
**Composition:** Overhead, very tight. The hands enter from one edge. Focus on the chalk mark.
**Format:** 4:5 vertical, or 16:9 horizontal if the gesture suits a wider crop.
**Why:** This is the *only* plate with a human element. It signals craft without resorting to a face. Will live mid-About page.

### Plate 5 — Wardrobe in winter light

**Subject:** Three coats — black, camel, navy — on wooden hangers, photographed from outside an opened wardrobe. North-facing daylight falls obliquely on the shoulders. The interior is slightly shadowed; the cream cabinet door catches the light.
**Composition:** Slight worm's-eye, looking up into the wardrobe. The coats occupy the centre vertical third.
**Format:** Vertical, 4:5 or 3:4.
**Why:** Mood-forward editorial plate. Will live on Journal index as an atmospheric counterpoint to the typography.

### Plate 6 — Folded knitwear, close

**Subject:** Tight crop of two stacked jumpers (cream over stone), a brass-buckle belt curled beside them on washed linen.
**Composition:** Tight overhead, knitwear filling two-thirds, belt curling into the bottom corner.
**Format:** Square or 4:5.
**Why:** Secondary placement — pricing-page section divider, About-page inline image.

### Plate 7 — A single coat at rest

**Subject:** A single dark wool coat on an unfinished oak hanger, against a cream wall. Slight shadow falling from upper left. No props.
**Composition:** Vertical portrait crop. The coat occupies the centre vertical third; cream wall above and below.
**Format:** 3:4 vertical.
**Why:** Essay opener for any stewardship-themed Journal piece. The "one good coat" image.

### Plate 8 — The Atelier (the studio itself)

**Subject:** The tools of considering: a fountain pen, a hand-bound notebook open to a blank page, a small brass paperclip, an unlit taper candle, a small glass bottle of ink. Arranged on the same linen surface as Plate 1, so the suite reads as one location.
**Composition:** Overhead, asymmetric — the notebook in the lower third, the pen across it, the candle vertical and slightly off-axis, the ink bottle in the upper right.
**Format:** Square.
**Why:** About page or Philosophy section. The conceptual self-portrait of the brand.

## 5. Styling & set

**Surfaces:**

- One large piece of washed natural linen, in oat or stone (about 2 × 1.5 m)
- One slab of honed pale limestone or marble (60 × 60 cm minimum)
- A section of unfinished or wax-finished oak — a small table, a board, or the floor of the studio if it is right
- A cream-painted wall flat for the upright shots

**Props (we will source or supply, by agreement):**

- Three folded jumpers in cream, stone, navy — high-quality knitwear, no visible branding (we have these or will provide)
- Three coats — black wool, camel, dark navy — on wooden hangers
- Wax-seal kit with the Atelier monogram (we will supply)
- Fountain pen (vintage if possible — a Montblanc Meisterstück, an old Parker, or a Pilot)
- Linen-bound notebook or ledger (Smythson, Choosing Keeping, or similar)
- Tailor's chalk, cloth measuring tape (in linen or cotton, not vinyl), brass thimble
- Brass paperclip(s), unlit ivory taper candle, small glass ink bottle
- Vegetable-tanned tan leather swatch, heathered grey wool swatch, oat linen swatch

**What not to include in frame:**

- Any branding, logo, or label
- Any electronics, modern packaging, or plastic
- Any pure white, primary colours, or saturated tones
- Anything shiny (polished gold, glossy varnish, chrome)
- Anything that reads as "lifestyle photography" — coffee cups, planners with handwritten notes, succulents, "flat lay aesthetic" tropes

## 6. Light

North-facing daylight only. No strobe. Reflectors permissible only to soften, never to fill aggressively. If the natural light fails on the day, a single tungsten softbox at ~5000K through a thick scrim, positioned to mimic window light from the upper left.

The mood is late afternoon in winter. Shadows are present and welcome. Frames may feel slightly underexposed in raw — we will lift in post, gently.

## 7. Post-production

- Colour graded toward warm cream, slightly desaturated. No clarity push. No sharpening beyond necessary.
- Skin tones in Plate 4 (hands) graded warm but not orange. Should read as "hands in soft daylight," not "tanned hands in flattering light."
- Brass tones must read as the brand brass (`#A8884C`–`#D4B378`), not as bright gold.
- Delivered as both 16-bit TIFFs (archival masters) and high-quality JPEGs at three sizes:
  - Master: 3000 px on the long edge
  - Web hero: 2400 px
  - Web inline: 1600 px
- Light grain texture welcome — film simulation (Portra 400 NC, Provia, or similar) preferred over digital cleanness. We are happy to shoot the day on film if the photographer prefers, with hybrid scanning.

## 8. Technical specifications

| Spec | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Medium | Digital medium-format preferred (Phase One, Fuji GFX, Hasselblad X); 35mm full-frame acceptable; film welcomed |
| Resolution | 3000 px minimum on the long edge for masters |
| Colour space | Adobe RGB (1998) for masters; sRGB for web deliverables |
| File format | 16-bit TIFF masters + JPEG web exports |
| Aspect ratios required | Square (1:1), vertical (4:5 and 3:4), one horizontal (16:9 or 21:9) — see shot list |

## 9. Licensing & usage

- **Scope:** Worldwide, perpetual, royalty-free, for use on the Atelier marketing site (myatelier.style), email, social media, press, and printed marketing materials.
- **Exclusivity:** Exclusive to Atelier for two years from delivery, then non-exclusive (photographer may add to portfolio at any time, including immediately).
- **Credit:** Photographer credited in site colophon and in any press use where standard.
- **Resale or sub-licensing:** Atelier may not resell or sub-license the imagery to third parties without written consent.

## Appendix — alternative path: commissioned line illustration

If a photography commission proves the wrong shape for this stage of the brand, the next-best path is a suite of small pen-and-ink line illustrations, executed in brass on cream. Eight to twelve illustrations at roughly £80–120 each (illustrator-dependent) gives the publication a visual signature no photograph can replicate — closer to a Penguin Classics drop cap or a *Toast* catalogue spot illustration than to magazine photography. The brief would name the same references (Toast, *Cereal*, *Wallpaper\** small spots) and require:

- Pen-and-brush ink, scanned at 600 DPI minimum, delivered as transparent PNGs
- Brass colour (`#A8884C`) baked in, or supplied as line art for the brand to tint
- Subjects: a folded jumper, a coat on a hanger, a wax seal, a fountain pen, a tailor's chalk, a thimble, a wooden hanger, an ink bottle
- The same licensing terms as above

This would cost £700–1,200 total and would be ready in three weeks rather than six.

The illustration path is the more distinctive option. The photography path is the more luxurious one. Either reads as premium when executed well; neither will if executed poorly. The choice is one of voice, not of quality.
